# Labor Financials — How IBM Apptio Costing Standard Addresses This TBM Capability

## The capability

Labor is typically the single largest and least transparent cost pool in a technology budget —
internal employees, contractors, offshore resources, and vendor staff augmentation all blend
together in most GL exports. Without a structured view of labor composition and cost, an
organization can't answer basic TBM questions: how much of our spend is internal vs. external?
Is our labor mix efficient? Where is that labor actually being consumed?

## How IBM Apptio Costing Standard covers it

The **Workforce** capability gives visibility into internal, external, contingent, and offshore
labor — composition, distribution by role and location, and the costs tied to labor planning and
management. Four out-of-the-box use cases build on each other in a deliberate sequence, per the
documentation's own framing:

1. **Labor Cost Analysis** — "Understanding total labor cost and its drivers is essential before
   allocation or benchmarking can be effective." This is the foundation, not an afterthought.
2. **Headcount Planning & Variance** — described as "the primary entry point for Workforce & Time
   Tracking": approved vs. actual headcount, to manage staffing and budget risk.
3. **Labor Mix Optimization** — once headcount is visible, balancing internal, external,
   offshore, and contingent labor for cost and efficiency.
4. **Labor Cost Allocation** — building on cost visibility to enable accountability across
   projects, applications, products, services, and platforms — the connective tissue back to every
   other TCO capability in this knowledge base (Application TCO, Product TCO, and Data Center TCO
   all depend on labor being allocated correctly upstream).

A closely related capability, **Time Tracking**, sits alongside Workforce specifically for
organizations that need effort-based (not assumption-based) labor allocation: "allocation of labor
costs based on reported time across projects and operational activities... using time-based data
rather than assumptions or static allocations." This matters for any client with a mature PMO that
already tracks time and wants that data to drive cost, not just project status.

Custom use cases extend this further into **Workforce Planning**, **Capacity & Demand Forecast**,
**Skills & Resource Allocation**, and **Labor Rate Benchmarking** — the last of which compares
internal and vendor labor rates against industry benchmarks to surface pricing inefficiencies.

## Why this matters in a client conversation

Labor Financials is worth positioning as a prerequisite, not a parallel workstream, when a client
asks for Application TCO, Product TCO, or any showback/chargeback capability — all of those depend
on labor being mapped correctly first (this is the same Labor Mapping workflow documented in the
Cost Pool to Resource Tower allocation brief). A client that skips investing here typically ends up
with technically-correct-looking TCO numbers built on a labor allocation that's actually a rough
guess.

The explicit sequencing in the documentation (cost visibility → headcount → mix optimization →
allocation) is also a useful scoping tool for a phased engagement: it maps cleanly onto a
crawl/walk/run maturity conversation rather than requiring all four use cases on day one.
