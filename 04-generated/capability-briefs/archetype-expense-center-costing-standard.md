# Technology Business Model Archetype: Expense Center — How IBM Apptio Costing Standard Helps a Client Move Off It

## The archetype

The TBM Council names Expense Center as the archetype no organization should aspire to: "The
expense center model is not appropriate for any corporate strategy today. It is the vestige of an
era when technology wasn't essential to business growth and transformation." Expense Center IT is
"characterized by a lack of service orientation" and funded by arbitrary formulas — a percentage
of revenue, headcount, or a baseline budget adjustment — none of which actually reflects business
need.

The TBM Council's own diagnostic of why this archetype fails is specific and maps directly onto
Costing Standard capabilities already covered in this knowledge base:

- **Create Transparency fails**: "IT leaders see their costs through the standard financial
  reporting model of the enterprise by relying on a structure of general ledger (GL) accounts and
  cost centers. They have few meaningful metrics to discuss cost and performance tradeoffs."
- **Deliver Value for Money fails**: benchmarking, if done at all, happens once every year or two;
  spending cuts are top-down with no service-level understanding.
- **Shape Business Demand fails**: "Business partners are not allocated costs at all or are
  allocated costs based on a predetermined rate... Cost allocations provide little incentive for a
  business unit to change its consumption."
- **Plan and Govern fails**: budgets are set by arbitrary formula and cut top-down "without any
  understanding of how services will be impacted afterwards."

## How IBM Apptio Costing Standard addresses each gap

This is close to a direct capability-by-capability match with what's already documented in this
knowledge base:

- Where Expense Center has "no meaningful metrics," **Cost Pool to Resource Tower allocation** and
  the **Cost Pools & Resource Towers (budget/forecast)** capability replace raw GL/cost-center
  reporting with a functional, TBM-standard structure — the first concrete transparency win.
- Where Expense Center benchmarks "once every one or two years, if at all," Costing Standard's
  **Cost Pool Analysis** use case benchmarks OpEx against revenue and per-user metrics on an
  ongoing basis (see the IT Financials capability brief), and Apptio Benchmarking extends that to
  peer comparison.
- Where Expense Center allocates costs "based on a predetermined rate" with no consumption
  incentive, Costing Standard's **Showback/Chargeback** capability (Business Unit and Services
  views) ties cost directly to actual consumption.
- Where Expense Center budgets by "arbitrary formula," the **IT Financials** capability's Budget
  Variance Analysis and Transaction-Level Insights replace top-down formula cuts with a
  transaction-traceable, governed budget process.

## Why this matters in a client conversation

A client that self-identifies as an Expense Center is, per the TBM Council, describing an
organization at existential risk of being outsourced or replaced — the document is blunt that
"expense centers are forced to evolve or are replaced." This gives Costing Standard a clear,
sequenced positioning: don't propose the full TBM Model on day one. Propose the specific
capabilities that directly answer the four named failures above, in roughly that order — cost
pool/tower transparency first (the client literally cannot see functional cost today), then
benchmarking, then showback, then governed budgeting. That sequence mirrors the TBM Council's own
stated path: "For expense center CIOs who are trying to migrate up the value chain, the service
provider business model is the logical starting point."
